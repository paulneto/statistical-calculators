T-Test

"The classic comparison tool"

What is it?

The t-test compares the average (mean) between two groups to see if they're statistically different. It's like asking: "Is Version A really better than Version B, or did I just get lucky?"

When to Use It:

  • ‚úì Comparing 2 groups (and only 2!)
  • ‚úì Your data is continuous (numbers like prices, time, ratings)
  • ‚úì Data is roughly normal-ish (bell curve-ish)
  • ‚úì You want to compare means

Three Flavors:

  • Independent: Two separate groups (Ad A vs Ad B)
  • Paired: Same group, before/after (Pre-campaign vs Post-campaign)
  • One-Sample: Your group vs a known value (Your NPS vs industry average of 50)

üé¨ Real Example:

Scenario: You're testing two email subject lines for your Black Friday campaign.

  • ‚Ä¢ Subject A: "50% Off Everything!" ‚Üí sent to 1,000 people ‚Üí avg. order value: $85
  • ‚Ä¢ Subject B: "Last Chance: Huge Savings!" ‚Üí sent to 1,000 people ‚Üí avg. order value: $92

Question: Is that $7 difference real, or just random luck?

Answer: Run an independent t-test! If p < 0.05, Subject B is legitimately better. üéâ

üìñ How to Read Results:

t-statistic: How many "standard errors" apart the groups are. Bigger = more different.
p-value: If p < 0.05, the difference is "statistically significant" (probably not random).
Cohen's d (effect size): How BIG is the difference? < 0.5 = small, 0.5-0.8 = medium, > 0.8 = large.
95% Confidence Interval: "We're 95% sure the true difference is between X and Y."

‚ö†Ô∏è Watch Out For:

  • ‚Ä¢ Only works for 2 groups ‚Äî if you have 3+, use ANOVA instead!
  • ‚Ä¢ Needs decent sample sizes (at least 30 per group is safer)
  • ‚Ä¢ Assumes data is roughly normal (not super skewed)
  • ‚Ä¢ Statistical significance ‚â† practical importance! A difference can be "significant" but tiny and meaningless for business.

ANOVA

"T-test's big brother for multiple groups"

What is it?

ANOVA (Analysis of Variance) compares means across 3 or more groups. It tells you: "Is at least one group different from the others?" Think of it as a t-test on steroids.

When to Use It:

  • ‚úì Comparing 3+ groups
  • ‚úì Continuous outcome variable (sales, satisfaction, etc.)
  • ‚úì Want to know if any groups differ
  • ‚úì All groups have similar variance

Two Types:

  • One-Way ANOVA: One factor (e.g., comparing 3 ad designs)
  • Two-Way ANOVA: Two factors at once (e.g., ad design √ó time of day)

üé¨ Real Example:

Scenario: You're testing 4 different promotional strategies for your loyalty program:

  • ‚Ä¢ Strategy A: 10% discount ‚Üí avg. new signups: 45/week
  • ‚Ä¢ Strategy B: Free shipping ‚Üí avg. new signups: 52/week
  • ‚Ä¢ Strategy C: Points multiplier ‚Üí avg. new signups: 61/week
  • ‚Ä¢ Strategy D: Exclusive access ‚Üí avg. new signups: 48/week

Question: Are these differences real or random noise?

Answer: Run one-way ANOVA! If F-statistic is significant (p < 0.05), at least one strategy works differently.

üìñ How to Read Results:

F-statistic: Ratio of between-group variance to within-group variance. Bigger = groups differ more.
p-value: If p < 0.05, at least one group is significantly different (but doesn't tell you which one!).
Eta-squared (Œ∑¬≤): Effect size. Shows % of variance explained by groups. 0.01 = small, 0.06 = medium, 0.14+ = large.
üîç Pro Tip: ANOVA only tells you "something is different" ‚Äî you need post-hoc tests (like Tukey's HSD) to figure out WHICH groups differ!

‚ö†Ô∏è Watch Out For:

  • ‚Ä¢ ANOVA doesn't tell you WHICH groups differ ‚Äî only that at least one does!
  • ‚Ä¢ Assumes equal variances across groups (Levene's test can check this)
  • ‚Ä¢ More sensitive to outliers than t-tests
  • ‚Ä¢ With many groups, you increase the chance of false positives (Type I error)

Chi-Square Test

"For when you're counting categories"

What is it?

Chi-square tests relationships between categorical variables (groups, types, categories). It's all about counts ‚Äî not averages! Think: "Do men and women prefer different products?"

When to Use It:

  • ‚úì Comparing categories (not numbers)
  • ‚úì Data is frequency counts (how many in each group)
  • ‚úì Want to test independence or goodness of fit
  • ‚úì Each cell has at least 5 expected observations

Two Flavors:

  • Goodness of Fit: Does your data match expected distribution?
  • Test of Independence: Are two categorical variables related?

üé¨ Real Example:

Scenario: You want to know if customer age group affects product preference.

Age Group Product A Product B Product C
18-25 120 45 35
26-40 85 110 55
41+ 40 75 90

Question: Is product choice independent of age, or are they related?

Answer: Chi-square test of independence! If p < 0.05, age and product preference ARE related.

üìñ How to Read Results:

œá¬≤ (Chi-square value): How much observed counts differ from expected. Bigger = more association.
p-value: If p < 0.05, the variables are significantly related (not independent).
Cram√©r's V (effect size): Strength of association. 0-0.1 = weak, 0.1-0.3 = moderate, 0.3+ = strong.
Degrees of freedom (df): Calculated as (rows - 1) √ó (columns - 1). Just needed for looking up critical values.

‚ö†Ô∏è Watch Out For:

  • ‚Ä¢ Only for categorical data ‚Äî can't use it on continuous variables!
  • ‚Ä¢ Needs at least 5 expected observations in each cell (use Fisher's exact test if cells are small)
  • ‚Ä¢ Doesn't show direction ‚Äî tells you IF there's a relationship, not HOW variables relate
  • ‚Ä¢ Sensitive to sample size ‚Äî huge samples make even tiny differences "significant"

Correlation

"Measuring how things move together"

What is it?

Correlation measures the strength and direction of the relationship between two continuous variables. Key word: relationship, not causation! Just because two things correlate doesn't mean one causes the other.

When to Use It:

  • ‚úì Both variables are continuous (numbers)
  • ‚úì Want to know if two things are related
  • ‚úì Not trying to predict one from the other (that's regression)
  • ‚úì Relationship is roughly linear (straight line-ish)

The r Value:

  • r = +1: Perfect positive correlation (as X ‚Üë, Y ‚Üë)
  • r = 0: No correlation (no relationship)
  • r = -1: Perfect negative correlation (as X ‚Üë, Y ‚Üì)
  • |r| > 0.7: Strong correlation
  • |r| = 0.3-0.7: Moderate correlation
  • |r| < 0.3: Weak correlation

üé¨ Real Example:

Scenario: You suspect that your advertising spend and sales revenue are related.

  • ‚Ä¢ Collect data for 12 months
  • ‚Ä¢ X-axis: Monthly ad spend ($1K, $2K, $3K, etc.)
  • ‚Ä¢ Y-axis: Monthly sales revenue

Question: Is there a relationship between ad spend and sales?

Answer: Calculate Pearson's r! If r = 0.85 (strong positive), more ad spend is associated with higher sales. If p < 0.05, the correlation is statistically significant (not due to chance).

üìñ How to Read Results:

r (Pearson's correlation): Ranges from -1 to +1. Sign shows direction, magnitude shows strength.
r¬≤ (R-squared): % of variance in Y explained by X. If r = 0.8, then r¬≤ = 0.64 = 64% explained.
p-value: If p < 0.05, the correlation is "real" (not random noise).
üö® BIG WARNING: Correlation ‚â† Causation! Just because ice cream sales and drowning rates correlate doesn't mean ice cream causes drowning. (Both increase in summer!)

‚ö†Ô∏è Watch Out For:

  • ‚Ä¢ Correlation DOES NOT imply causation! (Can't say this enough!)
  • ‚Ä¢ Outliers can dramatically affect r
  • ‚Ä¢ Only measures linear relationships (misses curves)
  • ‚Ä¢ Spurious correlations are everywhere ‚Äî always ask "does this make sense?"

Regression

"Predicting the future (sort of)"

What is it?

Regression lets you predict one variable from another. It's like correlation's practical cousin. Instead of just saying "these are related," regression says "if X increases by 1, Y will increase by [this much]."

When to Use It:

  • ‚úì Want to predict an outcome (Y) from predictors (X)
  • ‚úì Both variables are continuous
  • ‚úì Relationship is roughly linear
  • ‚úì Need to quantify how much X affects Y

The Equation:

Y = b‚ÇÄ + b‚ÇÅ(X)
  • Y: What you're predicting (dependent variable)
  • X: What you're using to predict (independent variable)
  • b‚ÇÄ: Intercept (Y when X = 0)
  • b‚ÇÅ: Slope (change in Y for each 1-unit increase in X)

üé¨ Real Example:

Scenario: You want to predict monthly sales based on your Facebook ad spend.

  • ‚Ä¢ Collected 10 months of data
  • ‚Ä¢ Run regression: Sales = $5,000 + $4.50(Ad Spend)

What this means:

  • ‚Ä¢ Intercept ($5,000): Base sales with zero ad spend
  • ‚Ä¢ Slope ($4.50): For every $1 spent on ads, sales increase by $4.50

Prediction: If you spend $1,000 on ads ‚Üí Expected sales = $5,000 + $4.50(1,000) = $9,500

üìñ How to Read Results:

Slope (b‚ÇÅ): The "bang for your buck." How much Y changes for each unit of X.
R¬≤ (R-squared): % of variance explained by the model. 0.75 = model explains 75% of variation in Y.
p-value for slope: If p < 0.05, X is a significant predictor of Y.
Standard error: How much predictions typically miss by. Lower = better predictions.

‚ö†Ô∏è Watch Out For:

  • ‚Ä¢ Don't extrapolate beyond your data! Model breaks down outside observed range.
  • ‚Ä¢ Outliers can throw off the entire line
  • ‚Ä¢ Just because model is significant doesn't mean it's useful (check R¬≤!)
  • ‚Ä¢ Correlation ‚â† Causation still applies! Regression doesn't prove X causes Y.

Mann-Whitney U Test

"When your data is weird"

What is it?

The Mann-Whitney U test is the non-parametric alternative to the t-test. It compares medians instead of means, and it works when your data is skewed, has outliers, or isn't normal.

When to Use It:

  • ‚úì Comparing 2 independent groups
  • ‚úì Data is skewed or has outliers
  • ‚úì Data is ordinal (rankings, Likert scales)
  • ‚úì Sample sizes are small
  • ‚úì T-test assumptions are violated

Key Difference from T-Test:

  • T-test: Compares means, assumes normal distribution
  • Mann-Whitney: Compares distributions/ranks, no normality assumption
  • Think of it as: "Does one group tend to have higher values than the other?"

üé¨ Real Example:

Scenario: You're comparing customer satisfaction ratings (1-5 stars) between two store locations.

  • ‚Ä¢ Store A: Mostly 3-4 stars, few outliers at 1 star
  • ‚Ä¢ Store B: More consistent 4-5 stars
  • ‚Ä¢ Data is ordinal (Likert scale) and skewed

Question: Is Store B significantly better?

Answer: Use Mann-Whitney U test instead of t-test! It's better suited for ordinal, non-normal data. If p < 0.05, Store B has significantly higher ratings.

üìñ How to Read Results:

U statistic: Counts how many times values from Group B exceed values from Group A. Smaller U = bigger difference.
Z-score: Standardized version of U. Used for larger samples (n > 20).
p-value: If p < 0.05, the distributions differ significantly.

‚ö†Ô∏è Watch Out For:

  • ‚Ä¢ Less powerful than t-test if data IS normal (harder to detect real differences)
  • ‚Ä¢ Only for 2 groups ‚Äî if you have 3+, use Kruskal-Wallis instead
  • ‚Ä¢ Tests if distributions differ, not just medians (can be significant even if medians are same!)

Z-Test for Proportions

"Comparing percentages like a pro"

What is it?

The Z-test for proportions compares percentages between two groups. Think: conversion rates, click-through rates, success rates ‚Äî anything that's "X out of Y people did something."

When to Use It:

  • ‚úì Comparing two proportions/percentages
  • ‚úì Data is binary (yes/no, click/no click)
  • ‚úì Large enough sample (at least 10 successes and failures in each group)
  • ‚úì Independent samples

Common Use Cases:

  • ‚Ä¢ A/B testing conversion rates
  • ‚Ä¢ Comparing click-through rates
  • ‚Ä¢ Survey response differences
  • ‚Ä¢ Win rates between strategies

üé¨ Real Example:

Scenario: You're testing two landing page designs for sign-ups.

  • ‚Ä¢ Page A: 1,200 visitors ‚Üí 84 sign-ups ‚Üí 7.0% conversion
  • ‚Ä¢ Page B: 1,200 visitors ‚Üí 108 sign-ups ‚Üí 9.0% conversion

Question: Is that 2% difference real or just luck?

Answer: Run a Z-test for proportions! If p < 0.05, Page B legitimately has a higher conversion rate. Ship it! üöÄ

üìñ How to Read Results:

Z-score: How many standard errors apart the proportions are. Bigger Z = more different.
p-value: If p < 0.05, the difference is statistically significant.
Difference in proportions: Absolute difference (e.g., 9.0% - 7.0% = 2.0% improvement).
üí° Pro Tip: Even if p < 0.05, ask: "Is this difference meaningful for business?" A 0.1% improvement might be "significant" but not worth the effort!

‚ö†Ô∏è Watch Out For:

  • ‚Ä¢ Needs large samples (at least 10 successes/failures per group)
  • ‚Ä¢ Small differences can be "significant" with huge samples but not actionable
  • ‚Ä¢ Assumes independence ‚Äî don't use for before/after on same people

Bayesian Statistics

"A different way of thinking about data"

What is it?

Bayesian statistics flips traditional stats on its head. Instead of asking "what's the probability of seeing this data IF my hypothesis is true?" (frequentist), it asks "what's the probability my hypothesis is true GIVEN the data I've seen?" Mind = blown. ü§Ø

Key Differences from Frequentist:

  • ‚úì Incorporates prior knowledge
  • ‚úì Updates beliefs as new data arrives
  • ‚úì Gives probability that hypothesis is true
  • ‚úì Can make decisions with less data
  • ‚úì More intuitive interpretation

The Core Equation:

Posterior = Prior √ó Likelihood
  • Prior: What you believed before seeing data
  • Likelihood: What the data says
  • Posterior: Your updated belief

üé¨ Real Example:

Scenario: You're running an A/B test for a new checkout flow.

  • ‚Ä¢ Prior belief: Based on past tests, you think conversion rate is around 5%
  • ‚Ä¢ Observed data: New flow gets 6.2% conversion (58 out of 935 users)
  • ‚Ä¢ Posterior belief: Combining prior + data, you now believe it's 5.8%

Bayesian Answer: "There's an 87% probability that Version B is better than Version A."

Frequentist Answer: "We reject the null hypothesis at p < 0.05."

See the difference? Bayesian gives you the probability directly ‚Äî way more useful for business decisions!

üìñ Key Concepts:

Prior Distribution: Your belief before seeing data. Can be "uninformative" (no strong opinion) or "informative" (based on past data).
Credible Interval (CI): Bayesian version of confidence interval. "95% CI [0.05, 0.08]" means "95% chance the true value is between 5% and 8%." (This is what people THINK confidence intervals mean!)
P(B > A): Direct probability that B is better than A. E.g., "82% chance B beats A."

üí° Why Marketers Love Bayesian:

  • ‚Ä¢ Actionable results: Get probability of success, not just "significant or not"
  • ‚Ä¢ Faster decisions: Can stop tests early when you have enough evidence
  • ‚Ä¢ Incorporates domain knowledge: Use past campaign data as priors
  • ‚Ä¢ Easier to explain: "87% chance B is better" > "p = 0.03 with 97% CI"

‚ö†Ô∏è Watch Out For:

  • ‚Ä¢ Prior choice matters! Different priors can give different results
  • ‚Ä¢ More complex to calculate (but our calculator does it for you!)
  • ‚Ä¢ Not universally accepted in academic journals (yet)
  • ‚Ä¢ Can be computationally intensive for complex models

üéì Final Exam Survival Tips

‚úÖ DO:

  • ‚Ä¢ Check your data type FIRST (continuous, categorical, ordinal?)
  • ‚Ä¢ Always look at effect sizes, not just p-values!
  • ‚Ä¢ Visualize your data before running tests
  • ‚Ä¢ Report confidence/credible intervals
  • ‚Ä¢ Ask: "Is this statistically AND practically significant?"
  • ‚Ä¢ Use the right test for your data (don't force it!)

‚ùå DON'T:

  • ‚Ä¢ Confuse correlation with causation
  • ‚Ä¢ Cherry-pick results until you get p < 0.05 (p-hacking!)
  • ‚Ä¢ Ignore assumptions (normality, independence, etc.)
  • ‚Ä¢ Run multiple tests without correcting for multiple comparisons
  • ‚Ä¢ Use t-test for 3+ groups (use ANOVA!)
  • ‚Ä¢ Treat "not significant" as "no effect" (absence of evidence ‚â† evidence of absence)

üéØ The Golden Rule:

Stats is a tool, not a magic 8-ball. Numbers can guide decisions, but they can't tell you what to do. Always combine statistical results with domain knowledge, business context, and common sense.

And remember: p < 0.05 doesn't mean your idea is good ‚Äî it just means it's probably not random. A tiny, meaningless effect can still be "statistically significant" with enough data!
